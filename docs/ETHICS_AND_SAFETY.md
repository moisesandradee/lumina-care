# Ethics and Safety — Lumina

## Preamble

Mental health technology occupies a uniquely sensitive intersection of human vulnerability, clinical responsibility, and technological power. We do not approach ethics in Lumina as a compliance exercise. We approach it as the foundational design constraint from which everything else follows.

If something is ethically problematic, it does not ship — regardless of its technical feasibility or commercial appeal.

---

## Core Ethical Commitments

### 1. The Clinician Is Always Accountable

Lumina does not make clinical decisions. It informs them.

Every risk score, every priority flag, every care recommendation generated by Lumina is explicitly advisory. The clinician remains the accountable professional. This is not only ethically correct — it is legally and regulatorily necessary.

**In practice:**
- All AI outputs are labeled "For clinical review"
- No AI output can be the sole basis for a documented clinical decision
- Override functionality is prominent, accessible, and frictionless
- Clinical overrides are logged but never penalized or surfaced as dissent

### 2. Patient Privacy Is Non-Negotiable

Patient data is among the most sensitive information that exists. Our handling of it reflects that.

**In practice:**
- All patient-identifying data is encrypted at rest and in transit
- Data minimization principle: we collect and process only what is clinically necessary
- No raw patient data is transmitted to external AI providers — only de-identified clinical signals
- Patients (or their guardians) have the right to request deletion of all stored data
- Data is never used for advertising, profiling, or non-clinical purposes

### 3. AI Must Be Explainable

A clinician should never be in the position of acting on an AI output they cannot explain or defend. Black-box AI has no place in clinical decision support.

**In practice:**
- Every AI output includes human-readable reasoning
- Confidence scores are always displayed
- Evidence references (clinical frameworks, validated instruments) are cited
- Model version is always recorded in the audit log

### 4. Acute Safety Risk Triggers Human Escalation — Always

If any signal in the Lumina system is consistent with acute suicidal ideation, active self-harm, or imminent danger, the response is always human escalation. There is no automated AI response to acute safety signals.

**In practice:**
- C-SSRS positive flags immediately surface to the assigned clinician with urgency indicators
- Care coordinators are notified in parallel
- Lumina does not attempt to respond to, manage, or "handle" acute safety events
- System documentation explicitly states that Lumina is not a crisis intervention tool

### 5. Lumina Does Not Diagnose

Psychiatric and psychological diagnosis requires clinical training, therapeutic relationship, and regulatory authorization. Lumina has none of these.

**In practice:**
- No output uses diagnostic language (DSM/ICD codes or diagnostic labels)
- Risk indicators are described as signals, patterns, and clinical flags — never diagnoses
- Prompts include explicit instructions against diagnostic framing
- Output validation schemas reject any response containing diagnostic terminology

### 6. Equity and Bias Awareness

AI systems trained on historical data inherit historical biases. In mental health, these biases can manifest as differential risk scoring across demographic groups — with potentially serious clinical consequences.

**In practice:**
- Initial deployment is scoped to structured assessment data (validated instruments) rather than unstructured text, reducing linguistic bias exposure
- Bias monitoring is a first-class roadmap item — aggregate demographic analysis of risk score distributions will be conducted prior to any broad deployment
- We will not deploy models we cannot audit for demographic performance disparities

## Clinical Boundaries

| Lumina does | Lumina does not |
|---|---|
| Surface psychosocial risk signals | Diagnose mental health conditions |
| Prioritize clinical attention | Replace clinical judgment |
| Detect care continuity gaps | Provide crisis intervention |
| Generate session-prep summaries | Write clinical notes autonomously |
| Recommend care actions (advisory) | Make treatment decisions |
| Aggregate team-level insights | Access individual data without authorization |
| Support early intervention | Serve as primary care for any patient |

## Data Governance

### Consent
- Patients must be informed that their care data is processed by a clinical intelligence platform
- Consent documentation is a prerequisite for patient enrollment in Lumina-enabled services
- Consent is granular: patients can opt out of AI-assisted analysis while remaining in the care program

### Retention
- Clinical data is retained for the duration legally required in the applicable jurisdiction
- AI output logs are retained for 7 years for clinical audit purposes
- Data deletion requests are processed within 30 days

### Access Control
- Role-based access: clinicians see their own patient data; coordinators see their assigned panel; directors see aggregate (de-identified) data
- Patient record access is logged with actor, timestamp, and clinical justification
- Privileged access (admin, engineering) to patient data requires dual authorization

### Third Parties
- No patient data is sold or licensed to third parties
- External AI providers (Anthropic) receive only anonymized, structured clinical signals
- All third-party processors are subject to Data Processing Agreements meeting applicable standards (LGPD, GDPR where applicable)

## Regulatory Positioning

Lumina is designed as a **clinical decision support tool**, not a medical device. This distinction matters:

- Clinical decision support tools that present patient-specific information to clinicians to inform (not replace) their decisions generally fall outside the regulatory scope of medical devices in most jurisdictions
- However, as the regulatory landscape for AI in healthcare evolves, Lumina's architecture is designed to accommodate reclassification
- We will seek formal regulatory guidance as the product matures and before any deployment in regulated markets

## Ethics Review Process

Any significant change to Lumina's AI capabilities — new model, new prompt architecture, new data type, new clinical use case — requires:

1. Written description of the proposed change and its clinical implications
2. Review by a mental health clinician with product knowledge
3. Review by a data privacy specialist
4. Sign-off from product leadership
5. Documentation in the ethics changelog

This is not a bureaucratic gate. It is a quality signal. Changes that cannot pass this review should not be in the product.
